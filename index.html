Key Figures

William Lloyd Garrison

William Lloyd Garrison

William Lloyd Garrison was the leader of the American Anti-Slavery Society (AASS) who encouraged female leadership and appointed seven female delegates to attend the first World Anti-Slavery Convention in 1840. These delegates were known as Garrisonians. Garrison urged many women to attend the World Slavery Conference. He encouraged American women to come to London, where Elizabeth Heyrick "enkindled a blaze which unfolded new scenes of action, and pointed new paths of duty."

Elizabeth Heyrick

Elizabeth Heyrick

Elizabeth Heyrick was an influential, radical reformer and writer with huge influence in the British movement for abolition in the United States. Heyrick organised successful boycotts on slave-produced goods and was a leading figure in the abstention movement. Furthermore, Heyrick writes of the importance of granting civil liberties and rights following emancipation. This then radical ideology was shared among many female reformers within the transatlantic sisterhood. One of Heyricks most significant contributions, was her emphasis on economic strategy, describing consumption as "a social act with consequences that resonated throughout the Atlantic world". Elizabeth Heyrick could recognise that what may seem like small actions made in London could have huge transatlantic repercussions.

Lucretia Mott

Lucretia Mott

Lucretia Mott was a quaker minister and leader of the American female delegates in London. Kathyrn Kish Sklar argues that the diary of Lucrecia Mott is one of the best lenses by which to study the female attendance of the World Anti-Slavery Convention. She wrote of her frustration at having spent a month crossing the Atlantic, and years defending their rights only to be turned away – it fuelled the fire of her motivation. Furthermore, it was at the first World Anti-Slavery convention in London where Lucretia Mott first met Elizabeth Cady Stanton. The two became close friends and went on to organise conventions for women's rights. This displays the transatlantic sisterhood as something of personal connection in addition to ideological exchange. London and the Anti-Slavery convention proved to be a catalytic event for civil rights in America.

Elizabeth Cady Stanton

Elizabeth Cady Stanton

Elizabeth Cady Stanton was a pioneering American suffragist and abolitionist whose encounter with exclusion at the 1840 World Anti-Slavery Convention in London proved transformative. Meeting Lucretia Mott there, the two forged a lifelong friendship that would go on to shape the American women's rights movement. Their shared experience of being barred from full participation crystallised Stanton's conviction that the causes of abolition and women's emancipation were inseparable.

Elizabeth Pease

Elizabeth Pease

Elizabeth Pease was an English abolitionist who was a Garrisonian before the convention in London. Pease attempted to organise separate female conferences to meet formally with their American sisters; however, she was obstructed by leaders of the British Foreign Anti-Slavery Society. She wrote of the American visit to London that "every obstacle was thrown in the way and no public opportunity was ever afforded them for a free interchange with their English sisters". Pease maintained extensive correspondence with American Garrisonians and understood that a radical transatlantic abolitionist network was needed in order to achieve an end to slavery.

Impact

I
Elizabeth Heyrick, Immediate, Not Gradual Abolition, republished Boston 1838

The transatlantic sisterhood brought British and American women together who pushed abolition in newer and more imaginative ways - many of these connections running directly through London. Elizabeth Heyrick was one of the most influential early voices within this movement, whose 1824 pamphlet 'Immediate, Not Gradual Abolition' called for the immediate end of slavery in the British West Indies and significantly shifted abolitionist thinking in Britain away from cautious gradualism.

What made Heyrick particularly significant in the bigger picture of the transatlantic sisterhood was how widely her message travelled. In 1836, her pamphlet was republished in the United States by the Philadelphia Ladies' Anti-Slavery Society, which believed that Heyrick's arguments were similarly relevant to slavery in the United States. These cases of British and American reading the same texts and sharing strategies evidences how abolitionist ideas circulated between women's networks both in Britain and the Untied States. Rather than acting separately, they were building a connected movement that approached abolition as a shared international responsibility.

Heyrick's emphasis on everyday activism, particularly through consumer boycotts became a model for many women's groups. She campaigned in Leicester to persuade grocers to stop selling slave-produced sugar and encouraged the public to pressure the slave economy through spending choices. Similar tactics to these started to be used by American free‑produce societies, showing how Heyrick's ideas spread across the Atlantic and shaped practical abolitionist work. This approach helped women gain political influence at a time when they were excluded from formal politics.

II
The Anti-Slavery Society Convention, London, 1840

London served as a key meeting place for many of these transatlantic exchanges. It served as home to the Ladies' London Emancipation Society, which formed in 1863 with the primary goal of supporting the abolition of slavery in the United States. Simultaneously, American activists like Sarah Parker Remond travelled to London to speak publicly against U.S. slavery. Her lectures were widely reported in the London press which helped bring American slavery experiences directly into British public debate and strengthened support for American abolition.

Another major London‑US link came in 1840, when the first World Anti‑Slavery Convention was held in London. This event brought together delegates from across the Atlantic but also helped expose deep gender inequalities. The American women who had travelled to London were refused full participation by the British and Foreign Anti‑Slavery Society. London newspapers covered the controversy, and the incident energised women on both sides of the Atlantic to organise more independently, and these shared experiences of exclusion became a foundation for stronger cooperation rather than division.

III

The transatlantic sisterhood ultimately created a network which linked London to United States anti‑slavery activism. It achieved this by sharing texts and organisational tactics. Heyrick helped set the moral tone by insisting on immediate emancipation, while women in both London and the U.S. carried these ideas across the Atlantic, turning abolition into a coordinated international effort rooted in moral conviction and collective action.
